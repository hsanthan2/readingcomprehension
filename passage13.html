On 26 December 2004, an earthquake rocked Sumatra, and triggered off a series of massive tsunamis that destroyed cities and seaside communities, killing great numbers of people. These effects are so devastating that rebuilding of the affected areas is still ongoing today. To date, the number of recorded deaths is more than a quarter million, and properties amounting to billions of dollars have been destroyed.

What is a tsunami? Well, it is actually a series of huge waves which are caused by an undersea earthquake or volcano. These gigantic waves can move at speeds of 720 kilometers per hour. As they approach the shore, they can reach heights of 30 meters, and crash onto land. Their speeds allow them to cause great destruction on the shore. After this disaster, efforts have been made to develop tsunami-warning systems, and programmes have been established to understand tsunamis better. These programmes are being used to educate people on what they can do if they should ever be hit by a tsunami.

Earthquakes often come together with volcanic eruptions. In late 1984, strong earthquakes began shaking the Nevado del Ruiz volcano in Columbia every day. On November 14, 1985, it erupted. A nearby river became a sea of mud that buried four towns. This disaster killed more than 2100 people.

People who live in earthquake zones should expect earthquakes. They should be prepared to protect their lives and properties. They must build safer buildings and roads. Seismologists are trying to find ways to use knowledge about earthquakes to save lives.
